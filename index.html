The method in four ideas

Most people don't fail at French because it's hard — they fail because they study passively, never speak, and quit. AI fixes the first two. You handle the third with a routine.

1

AI is an amplifier, not the engine

AI gives you unlimited reps and instant feedback. But it can be over-lenient and occasionally wrong — so pair it with real input and a human now and then.

2

Speaking is the bottleneck AI finally solves

The thing self-learners skip is talking. An AI voice tutor is patient, judgement-free and available 24/7 — so you actually produce the language every day.

3

Immersion is the cheat code

Switch your phone, your shows and your inner monologue to French. "Try first, then translate" — the struggle is where learning happens.

4

Consistency beats intensity

One focused hour every day beats a seven-hour weekend cram. Small, daily, measured — that's how the hours add up to fluency.

Set your target

French levels follow the CEFR scale. Pick a goal, then aim your hours at it. Here's roughly what each level means and how long it takes from zero.

🛂 Aiming for French citizenship?

Since 1 January 2026, naturalisation requires B2 (spoken and written) — up from B1 — plus a new civic test (40 multiple-choice questions, pass mark 32/40). The smart proof is the DELF B2 diploma: unlike the TCF/TEF tests (valid only 2 years), the DELF diploma is permanent — earn it once, banked for life. See the certification path →

⚠️ The AI traps — and how to beat them

  • False fluency: lenient speech recognition may not catch your errors. Counter it with a weekly human session and demanding prompts ("correct me strictly").
  • Hallucinated French: AI is rarely but occasionally wrong. Anchor your intuition with real input (native podcasts, TV) so you can sense when something's off.
  • App-hopping: three half-used apps beat nobody — but one used daily beats three. Pick your stack and stick to it.

The AI prompt cookbook

Copy any of these into ChatGPT or Claude to turn it into a tutor, a role-play partner, an exam examiner or a flashcard factory. Replace the [bracketed] bits with your level and topic.

Your 24/7 French tutor

You are my patient French tutor. I'm level [A2]. Speak mostly in simple French. After each of my messages: (1) reply naturally, (2) gently correct my mistakes with a one-line "why", (3) offer one better phrase I could have used. Keep me talking by ending with a question. Don't switch to English unless I'm totally stuck.

Café role-play

Role-play: you are a waiter in a Paris café and I'm ordering. Stay in French at my level [A2]. If I get stuck, offer me the phrase. At the end, list my 3 most useful corrections and 3 new words I should remember.

Préfecture / citizenship interview

Simulate a French naturalisation interview at the préfecture, in French. Ask me typical questions about my life in France, my integration, and French values. Keep it realistic. Afterwards, give feedback on my fluency, errors, and what to improve before the real thing.

Correct my essay (DELF B2)

Here is my French text: [paste]. Correct it and show a 3-column table: my version | correction | why. Then rewrite the whole thing cleanly at solid B2 level, and give it a DELF-style mark out of 25 with one tip.

Graded reader at my level

Write a 150-word French story at level [B1] about [topic]. Use roughly 90% common words and 10% new vocabulary. After the story, list the new words with their English meanings and an example sentence each.

Anki flashcards (CSV)

From this text [paste], extract the 15 most useful words and phrases for a learner. Output ONLY a CSV with columns: french,english,example_sentence. No extra commentary.

Grammar explainer + quiz

Explain [the subjunctive] simply, in English, with 5 clear French example sentences. Then give me a 3-question quiz and wait for my answers before marking them.

English → French daily drill

Give me 5 English sentences at level [A2] to translate into French. After I answer, mark each one, correct my errors, and explain the key mistake in one line. Then give me 5 slightly harder ones.

DELF B2 oral simulation

Act as a DELF B2 oral examiner. Give me a short French article on a current topic. I'll argue a position for ~3 minutes (I'll type it). Then ask me 2 challenging follow-up questions and debate me. Finally, score me against the DELF B2 speaking grid and list 3 fixes.

See your level — rewrite 3 ways

Rewrite this idea three ways and label each — A2, B1, and B2 — so I can see how the same meaning sounds at each level: [your sentence].

Comprehensible-input chat

Let's have a French conversation about [topic]. Use only vocabulary a [A2] learner would know. If you must use a harder word, put the English in brackets right after. Ask me one question at a time.

Pronunciation coach

I struggle with the French sounds [u vs ou], [nasal vowels], and the [r]. Give me 8 minimal-pair words for each, a simple tip for the mouth/tongue position, and 3 tongue-twisters to practise. (Then I'll practise out loud with a voice app.)

Tip: for spoken practice, paste these into ChatGPT voice or a dedicated app like Langua. Claude is great for the text-based ones (essay correction, flashcards, grammar).

💶 If you're in France — funding hacks

  • CPF (Compte Personnel de Formation): if you're on a French payroll you accrue ~€500/year that can pay for a course and the DELF exam.
  • Employer learning budget: many companies reimburse language classes — just ask.
  • OFII / CIR: some residence permits come with free state French classes (depends on your permit type).
  • Cours Municipaux: Paris adult-education courses are the best value going (~€50–350/term).

Certification path

When you're ready, prove your level. For most goals — work, immigration, citizenship — the DELF B2 is the diploma to get.

Recommended

DELF B2 — the diploma

  • Permanent — never expires
  • ~€150–280, once
  • 4 papers: listening, reading, writing, speaking
  • Accepted for citizenship & residence

TCF / TEF (IRN) — the test

  • Valid only 2 years
  • Fine if you need a result fast
  • Re-take if it lapses before you apply

Strategy: because the DELF diploma is permanent, earn it as soon as you're genuinely ready — it'll still be valid years later, and it hedges against future rule changes.

🛂 Citizenship: don't forget the rest

B2 is necessary but not sufficient. You also need ~5 years' continuous residence (some reductions apply), the new civic test (40 QCM, 32/40 to pass), a clean tax/residence record, and an assimilation interview in French. Always check the latest on service-public.fr before filing.

A worked example

Theory is cheap. Here's the plan applied to two real beginners in Paris — the same couple this playbook grew out of.

Ekta & Kartik moved to Paris and both start as near-beginners — Kartik's still at A1 after years because he lives and works in English and leans on translation apps. Their goals differ: Ekta wants B2 for herself; Kartik wants citizenship.

  • The diagnosis: the translation crutch is why progress stalled. Fix #1 is free — "try first, then translate," and make home a French zone.
  • The reframe: Kartik's 5-year residency lands in November 2026, so the residency box ticks itself — the only real constraint is reaching a genuine B2. No clock to beat, so build durably.
  • The plan: both take the same beginner course together (built-in study partners), do daily AI speaking practice, and target the DELF B2 around late 2027. Civic test + paperwork slot in near the end.
  • The funding: CPF + a possible employer stipend cover most costs.

The takeaway for anyone: match the plan to your real constraint. Sometimes it's time, sometimes it's a habit — name it, then aim your hours.
